Dividing attention hurts learning in adults but not children

Marlie Tandoc, Bharat Nadendla, Amy Finn
January, 2017
PDF Cite Project Slides Custom Link!
Made in R (ggplot2) and Illustrator

Abstract

Across two experiments we show that learning in childhood is fundamentally different from learning in adulthood. For adults, learning suffers when their attention is divided. For children, remarkably, learning is the same regardless of whether their attention is divided or not. And while children’s learning is expectedly worse than adults’ when attention is undivided, children learn just as well as adults—and sometimes even better—when attention is divided. These results suggest that a “sponge” may indeed be a good metaphor for learning during childhood. Children appear to take things in regardless of whether they are trying to or not.
